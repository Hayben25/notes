- Recall Green's Theorem:$$\oint_C\overrightarrow{h}(\overrightarrow r)\cdot d\overrightarrow r=\int_C P(x,y)dx+Q(x,y)dy=\iint_\Omega(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y})dxdy$$
- In vector terms, Green's Theorem can be written as$$\iint_\Omega(\nabla\cdot v)dxdy=\oint_C(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})ds$$
	- ($\overrightarrow{n}$ is the outer normal)
	- $\nabla \cdot \overrightarrow{v}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(Q)+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(P)=Q_x-P_y$ 
	- $ds=||\overrightarrow{r}(\omega)||du$
	- We want to increase dimensions 2D $\rightarrow$ 3D
		- $\Omega\rightarrow T$ a solid
		- $dxdy\rightarrow dxdydz$
		- $C\rightarrow S$ a boundary surface
		- $ds\rightarrow d\sigma$
	- In higher dimensions this gives $$\iiint_T(\nabla\cdot\overrightarrow{v})dxdydz=\iint_S(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})d\sigma$$
		- This statement is the Divergence Theorem
		- What is $\iint_S(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})d\sigma$
		- This is the flux of $\overrightarrow{v}$ through $S$
			- (by direct calculation) That is by summing amount of $\overrightarrow{v}$ pointing out of $S$ 
		- Recall from section 11.4 any $\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{u}$ gives $comp_u\overrightarrow{v}$ the amount of $\overrightarrow{v}$ in $\overrightarrow{u}$ direction

## Examples

1. Use the divergence theorem to find the total flux out of the solid $x^2+y^2\le4,\ 0\le z\le 1$ given $\overrightarrow{v}(x,y,z)=x\hat{i}+3y^2\hat{j}+2z^2\hat{k}$
	- We want flux of $\overrightarrow{v}$ through $S$, that is $\iint_S(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})d\sigma$ we prefer to find $\iiint_T div(\overrightarrow{v})dxdydz$
		- Not that direct calculation requires 3 surface integral
	- $div(\overrightarrow{v})=\nabla\cdot\overrightarrow{v}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}x+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}3y^2+\frac{\partial}{\partial z}2z^2=1+6y+4z$
	- $T:\ 0\le\theta\le 2\pi,\ 0\le r\le 2,\ 0\le z\le1$
	- $$Flux=\iiint_Tdiv(\overrightarrow{v})dxdydz=\int_0^{2\pi}\int^2_0\int_0^1=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2[rz+6r^2zsin\theta_2rz^2]_0^1drd\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2(3r+6r^2sin\theta)drd\theta=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^23rdrd\theta=12\pi$$
2. Use the divergence theorem to find the total flux out of the solid:$$0\le x\le1,\ 0\le y\le1-x,\ 0\le z\le 1-x-y$$
	- given $\overrightarrow{v}(x,y,z)=2x^2\hat{i}+4xy\hat{j}-4xz\hat{k}$
	- What is T?
		- Tetrahedron in 1$^{st}$ ocatant
	- $S$ has 3 sides $\implies$ 4 parameterizations, 3 surface integrals$$Flux=\iint_{S_1}(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})d\sigma+\dots$$
	- $div(\overrightarrow{v})=\nabla\cdot\overrightarrow{v}=4x$$$Flux=\iiint_T3xdxdydz=\int_0^1$$
3. Use the divergence theorem to find $\iint_S(\overrightarrow v\cdot \overrightarrow n)d\sigma$ for $\overrightarrow{v}(x,y,z)=(z+2x)\hat{i}+(4y+z^2)\hat j+(y+2z)\hat{k}$ on the surface $S$ of the solid $T:x^2+y^2\le 1,\ 0\le z\le 4$
	- $$Flux=\iint_S(\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n})d\sigma=\iiint_Tdiv(\overrightarrow{v})dxdydz\implies div(\overrightarrow{v})=8$$
	- $$Flux=32\pi$$
