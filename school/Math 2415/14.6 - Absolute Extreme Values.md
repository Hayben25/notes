How did we find absolute extreme values in Calc 1?
- 1$^{st}$ or 2$^{nd}$ derivative test
- Find critical numbers $C\in (a,b)$
- $f'(c)=0$ or DNE
- Find $f(a)$, $f(b)$, & $f(c)$ for each c
- The largest is max and smallest is min

## Calc 3 way
- Definition 1 Let *f* be a function of several variables with domain *D*:
	- *f* takes on an *absolute maximum* at $x_0$ if
		- $f(x_0)\ge f(x)$ for all $x\in D$
	- *f* takes on an *absolute minimum* at $x_0$ if
		- $f(x_0)\le f(x)$ for all $x\in D$
- Definition 1 (Bounded Set) A set *S* (of the real line, the plane, or 3-space) is *bounded* if there exists a positive real number *M* such that
	- $||x||\le M$ for all $x\in S$
	- Thus a set *S* in the plane or 3-space is bounded $\iff$ it can be contained in a ball of radius *M*
- Theorem 1 (Extreme Value Theorem) If *f* is continuous on a closed and bounded set *D* then *f* takes on an absolute max and min. An abs extrema will occur either at a critical point in interior of *D* or at a point on the boundary of *D*
- Here's what we do:
	1. Find the cp in the interior of *D*
	2. Find the extreme points on the boundary of *D*
	3. Evaluate *f* at the points found in Steps 1 and 2
	4. The largest of the numbers is abs max and the smallest is the abs min

## Examples

1. Find the absolute extreme values taken on by *f* on the set *D*
	- $f(x,y)=x^2+y^2+3xy+2$, $D=\{(x,y): x^2+y^2 \le 4\}$
	- $\nabla f(x,y)=(2x+3y,2y+3x)$
		- Set $\nabla f(x,y)=\overrightarrow{0}$
		- $\implies (x,y)=(0,0)\in D$
	- Boundary: Circle$x^2+y^2=4\implies x=2cos(t)$, $y=2sin(t)$, $t\in [0,2\pi]$
		- Here we collect all boundary points and look at *f*
		- Let $g(t)=f(x(t),y(t))=f(2cos(t),2sin(t))=4+3*2cos(t)*2sin(t)+2$
			- So $g(t)=6+12cos\ tsin\ t$ with $t\in [0,2\pi]$
		- Our task, optimize *g* on $t\in [0,2\pi]$, this is a calc 1 problem
		- Note that you must always check $g(t)$ at the bondairy of $[0,2\pi]$ at $t=0$, $t=2\pi$ we get x=2, y=0
		- $g'(t)=0+12cos^2t-12sin^2t=12(cos^2t+sin^2t)=0\implies cos^2t=sin^2t\implies sint=cost$
			- $t=\frac{\pi}{4},\frac{3\pi}{4},\frac{7\pi}{4},\frac{5\pi}{4}$
		- $(x,y)=(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2}),(-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2}),(-\sqrt{2},-\sqrt{2}),(\sqrt{2},-\sqrt{2})$
	- Plug in for all values (the ones above and (0,0) from the top and (2,0) from the endpoints of the boundary of the circle)
	- Abs max is $f(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2})=f(-\sqrt{2},-\sqrt{2})=12$
	- Abs min is $f(-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2})=f(-\sqrt{2},\sqrt{2})=0$
2. Find the absolute extreme values of the function $f(x,y) = 4xy-x^2-y^2-6x$ on the triangular region $D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 2, 0\le y\le 3x\}$
	- $\nabla f(x,y)=(4y-2x-6,4x-2y)=\overrightarrow{0}$
		- $x=1,y=2$ point $(1,2)\in D$
	- Boundary $\partial D$ has 3 parts
		- $c_1:(0,0)\rightarrow(2,0)$
			- $x=0+t(2-0)$, $y=0+t(0-0)$
			- $x=2t$, $y=0$, $t\in [0,1]$
			- Let $f_1(t)=f(2t,0)=-4t^2-12t$ on $t\in [0,1]$
			- $f'_1(t)=-8t-12=0\implies t=-\frac{3}{2},\ t\notin[0,1]\implies$ no points other than $r_1(0)=(0,0)$ and $r_1(1)=(2,0)$
		- We will use a shortcut parameterization for $c_2$ and $c_3$
		- $c_2:(2,0)\rightarrow(2,6)$
			- segment of the line x=2 $y\in [0,6]$
			- Let $f_2(y)=f(2,y)=8y-4-y^2-12=8y-y^2-16$ on $y\in [0,6]$
			- $f'_2(y)=8-2y=0\implies y=4\in [0,4]\implies check\ (2,4)\in D$
		- $c_3:(0,0)\rightarrow(2,6)$
			- Line segment $y=3x$, $x\in[0,2]$
			- Let $f_3(x)=f(x,3x)=12x^2-x^2-9x^2-6x=2x^2-6x$ on $x\in[0,2]$
			- $f'_3(x)=4x-6=0\implies x=\frac{3}{2}\in[0,2]\implies$ check $(\frac{3}{2},\frac{9}{2})$ 
		- Plug $(1,2),(0,0),(2,0),(2,6),(2,4),(\frac{3}{2},\frac{9}{2})$ for $f(x,y)$
			- abs max @ $f(0,0)=f(2,4)=0$
			- abs min @ $f(2,0)=-16$
