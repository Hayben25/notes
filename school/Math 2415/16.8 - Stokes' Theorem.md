- Let's revisit Green's Theorem:$$\oint_C\overrightarrow{h}(\overrightarrow r)\cdot d\overrightarrow r=\int_C P(x,y)dx+Q(x,y)dy=\iint_\Omega(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y})dxdy$$
- If we let $\overrightarrow{v}=P\hat{i}+Q\hat{j}+R\hat{k}$
	- $(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\hat{k}=\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$
	- What is the significance of $\hat{k}$ in a curve $C$
		- $\hat{k}$ is the unit normal to xy-plane
- An if $S$ is a flat surface in space bounded by a Jordan curve and $\overrightarrow{v}$ is continuously differentiable on $S$, we have$$\iiint_S[(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow{n}]d\sigma=\oint\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot d\overrightarrow{r}$$

## Examples

1. Let $S$ be the lower half of the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$ and take $\overrightarrow{n}$ as the outer unit normal. Use Stokes' theorem to find $\iiint_S[(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow n]d\sigma$ given $\overrightarrow{v}=2z^2\hat{i}+4x\hat{j}-4y^2\hat{k}$
	- We want $\iint[(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow{n}]d\sigma$ so we use $\oint_C\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow(r))d\overrightarrow{r}$
	- What is $S$? $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$, $z\le0$
	- What is $C$? $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$, meets $z=0$
		- $C:\ x^2+y^2=4,\ z=0$
	- We need $\overrightarrow{r}(u)$ for $C$ w/ orientation set by $\overrightarrow{n}$. Since $\overrightarrow{n}$ is into page, we need clockwise orientation as viewed.
		- $\overrightarrow{r}(u)=(2cos(u),-2sin(u),0),\ u\in[0,2\pi]$
	- $\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r}(u))=\overrightarrow{v}(2cos(u),-2sin(u),0)=0,3cos(u),4\cdot2^3sin^3(u)$
		- $\overrightarrow r'(u)=(-2sin(u),-2cos(u),0)$
		- $\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot \overrightarrow r'=-16cos^2(u)$
	- $$\iint_S[\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v}\cdot\overrightarrow{n}]d\sigma=\oint_C\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot d\overrightarrow{r}=-\int_0^{2\pi}-16cos^2(u)du=-16\cdot\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}[1+cos(2u)]du$$
2. Let $S$ be the portion of $z=9-x^2-y^2$ above the xy-plane. Use Stokes' theorem to find $\iiint_S(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow{n}d\sigma$ given $\overrightarrow{v}(x,y,z)=y\hat{i}-x\hat{j}+z\hat{k}$
	- $\iiint_S[(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow{n}]d\sigma=\oint_C\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot d\overrightarrow{r}$
	- What is $S$?
		- $z=9-x^2-y^2$ with $z\ge 0$
	- What is $C$?
		- $z=9-x^2-y^2$ meets $z=0\implies C:x^2+y^2=9,\ z=0$
	- What is $\overrightarrow{n}$?
		- Default is the outer (here outer is upper)
		- So $\overrightarrow{n}$ is out of the page
		- Thus we need a ccw parameterize as drawn or viewed
	- Parameterize
		- $x=3cos(u)$
		- $y=3sin(u)$
		- $z=0$
		- $u\in[0,2\pi]$
	- $\overrightarrow{r}(u)=(3cos(u), 3sin(u),0)$
		- $\nabla(\overrightarrow{r}(u))=(3sin(u),-3cos(u),0)$
		- $\nabla\overrightarrow{r}\cdot\overrightarrow{r}'=3^2cos(u)+3^2sin(u)+0=9$
	- $$\int_0^{2\pi}-9du=-9\cdot2\pi=-18\pi$$
3. Let $S$ be the portion of $y=z^2+x^2$ where $y\le4$. Use Stokes' theorem to find $\iiint_S(\nabla\times\overrightarrow{v})\cdot\overrightarrow{n}d\sigma$ given $\overrightarrow{v}(x,y,z)=y\hat{i}-x\hat{j}+z\hat{k}$. Use inner normal
	- What is $S$?
		- $y=z^2+x^2$ w/ $y\le 4$
	- What is $C$?
		- $C:x^2+z^2$ meets $y=4$
		- $C: x^2+z^2=4,\ y=4$
	- What is $\overrightarrow{n}$?
		- $\overrightarrow{n}$ is inner and $S$ is on our side of $C$, so $\overrightarrow{n}$ is away from use
		- Thus we need a cw parameterization as drawn
	- $\overrightarrow{r}(u)=(2cos(u),4,-2sin(u))\ u\in[0,2\pi]$
		- $\nabla\overrightarrow{r}=(-2sin(u),,-2cos(u))$
		- $\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot\overrightarrow{r}=-8sin(u)+4sin(u)cos(u)$ 
	- $$\oint_C\overrightarrow{v}(\overrightarrow{r})\cdot d\overrightarrow{r}=\int_0^{2\pi}[-8sin(u)+4sin(u)cos(u)]du=0$$
